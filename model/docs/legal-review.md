## Detailed Analysis

### GitHub PR Reviews

Pull request metadata (state, review decisions, CI conclusions, timestamps, file counts, author/reviewer usernames) are **facts about events**, not creative works. In US copyright law, facts are not copyrightable (*Feist Publications v. Rural Telephone Service*, 1991). The arrangement/selection of these facts into our 8-feature vector is our own original work.

Repository-specific open-source licenses apply to the **code** in those repositories, not to the PR metadata. Using PR metadata to train a governance model does not copy, distribute, or create derivative works of the licensed code.

**Risk: Low.** Continue scaling.

### GAL Session Exports

We own this data. It was generated by our systems and exported via our API.

**Risk: None.** Primary training source.

### Kaggle Jigsaw Toxicity

CC0 is a public domain dedication — the licensor waives all copyright and related rights. From a purely legal perspective, this data is the safest possible source.

However, **this data is not governance-relevant.** It classifies text toxicity, not process decisions. Training a governance model on toxicity data undermines our paper's thesis that "governance decisions are not content safety classifications."

**Recommendation: Do not use for training.** Keep the adapter as a reference but flag as not-for-training in documentation.

### HuggingFace Civil Comments

Apache 2.0 is a permissive license that allows use, modification, and distribution with minimal conditions (attribution, notice of changes). It does not impose copyleft or share-alike obligations. Training an ML model on Apache 2.0 data does not require the model to be Apache 2.0 licensed.

However, same signal problem as Kaggle: **this is content toxicity data, not governance data.**

**Recommendation: Do not use for training.** Same rationale as Kaggle.

### Stack Overflow Data Dump (CC BY-SA 4.0)

This is the legally interesting case. CC BY-SA 4.0 Section 2(a)(1)(A) provides a license to "reproduce and Share the Licensed Material, in whole or in part." Section 2(a)(1)(B) allows "produce, reproduce, and Share Adapted Material." **Adapted Material** is defined as material "in which the Licensed Material is translated, altered, arranged, transformed, or otherwise modified in a manner requiring permission under Copyright."

Key legal analysis from Szkalej & Senftleben (JIPITEC, 2024) and IVIR (2024):

1. **A trained model is likely not "Adapted Material."** A collection of weights and vectors derived from statistical analysis of many works does not constitute a translation, alteration, or transformation "requiring permission under Copyright" — it's a separate artifact. The SA obligation chain breaks at the model level.

2. **US fair use likely applies.** *Bartz v. Anthropic* (2025) found AI training "spectacularly transformative" fair use. Even plain copyright cannot restrict this use; a CC license cannot add restrictions beyond copyright.

3. **EU TDM exception may override.** Articles 3-4 of the CDSMD provide text and data mining exceptions. Unless the licensor actively opted out (unlikely for Stack Exchange, which published these dumps publicly), SA obligations may be unenforceable for ML training.

4. **Contract law path exists but is untested.** An opt-out → contractual chain could theoretically preserve SA obligations, but this requires active rights reservation by the licensor and acceptance by the licensee. Stack Exchange did not do this.

**Practical assessment:** The risk of CC BY-SA 4.0 "tainting" our model is low. The more significant concern is that Stack Overflow moderation data is closer to governance than toxicity, but still a different domain (community moderation vs. AI agent oversight). Use with documented rationale and accept the residual legal uncertainty.

**Recommendation: Usable with caution.** Keep in pipeline but at lower priority than GitHub and session data. Do not make it the majority of the training corpus.

## Principles Going Forward

1. **Prefer data we own or generate.** GAL sessions and synthetic governance scenarios are category-appropriate and risk-free.
2. **Prefer factual/event data over creative works.** PR metadata, CI outcomes, review decisions — facts, not expression.
3. **Synthetic data is the competitor pattern.** Llama Guard, ShieldGemma, and WildGuard all train on purpose-built synthetic data with human-validated labels. This is the direction to invest in.
4. **Document provenance.** Every training example should trace back to its source with license metadata. The curation pipeline already supports this via the source registry and corpus manifest.
5. **When in doubt, leave it out.** A smaller corpus of legitimate governance data beats a larger corpus of repurposed toxicity data.
